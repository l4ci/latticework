# Tuckman's Stages of Group Development: stages, behaviors, leadership, and pitfalls

Bruce Tuckman (1965, with Adjourning added 1977) holds that teams develop through predictable stages. Performance is not flat: it dips in Storming before it rises. Each stage has its own behaviors, its own team mood, the leadership style it needs, and a specific bar for advancing to the next. The work is to locate the stage from evidence and apply the matching support, not to expect Performing prematurely or skip the conflict of Storming.

The five stages run Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing, Adjourning. They are not strictly one-way: a team can regress when membership, leadership, or goals change.

## Scoring a stage

Tag each stage assessment so the match can be ranked rather than asserted:

- **Match** (1 to 5): how well the team's observable behaviors fit this stage's markers.
- **Confidence**: high, medium, or low, based on evidence. Lower it when observation is thin or secondhand rather than inventing certainty.
- **Regression signal**: note any sign the team has dropped back into this stage after a change (new members, new leader, new goal, reorg).

Ground every claim in observable behavior (how meetings run, how conflict surfaces or hides, how decisions get made, tenure, recent changes), not in how the team describes itself.

## The five stages

### 1. Forming
**Behaviors.** Members are polite, tentative, and anxious. They depend on the leader for direction. Roles, goals, and ways of working are unclear. Little real work happens; energy goes to orientation and testing what is acceptable. Disagreement is avoided.
**Team mood.** Excitement mixed with uncertainty; eagerness to be accepted.
**Leadership focus.** Direct. Set and clarify goals, roles, scope, and expectations. Give structure. Make it safe to ask questions.
**Advance when.** Goals and roles are understood well enough that members start to act on their own views, which surfaces the differences that begin Storming.

### 2. Storming
**Behaviors.** Conflict and friction. Members jockey for position, challenge authority, and contest the goal, the approach, and each other. Frustration is high, cliques form, and some members withdraw. This is the hardest stage and where most teams stall or get stuck.
**Team mood.** Frustration, tension, sometimes resentment; questioning whether the team can work.
**Leadership focus.** Coach. Surface conflict and normalize it as part of forming a team, do not suppress it. Build processes for decisions and disagreement. Build trust. Keep the team anchored to its shared goal while differences get worked out.
**Advance when.** The team works through its conflicts, accepts differences, and agrees on how it will operate, rather than burying disagreement or fragmenting.

### 3. Norming
**Behaviors.** Norms and cohesion form. Roles settle, trust and commitment build, members give and take constructive feedback. The team starts to function as a unit and gets real work done.
**Team mood.** Relief, belonging, growing confidence.
**Leadership focus.** Facilitate. Share decisions, reinforce the norms the team is building, resolve the conflicts that still surface, and step back as the team takes ownership.
**Advance when.** The team is reliably productive, self-correcting, and ready to operate with less direction.

### 4. Performing
**Behaviors.** High autonomy and high performance. The team self-organizes, resolves its own conflicts, and adapts without much oversight. It runs itself and delivers.
**Team mood.** Confidence, mutual trust, motivation.
**Leadership focus.** Delegate. Set direction and remove obstacles, then get out of the way. Stretch the team with harder goals and develop members.
**Advance when.** The work concludes or the team is disbanded or reconstituted, which moves it to Adjourning.

### 5. Adjourning (Mourning)
**Behaviors.** The team disbands or transitions to new work. Tasks wind down; members disengage. A sense of loss can accompany the breakup of a team that worked well.
**Team mood.** Pride in what was done, mixed with sadness or anxiety about the ending.
**Leadership focus.** Close well. Recognize contributions, mark the ending, capture learnings for the next team, and help members transition.

## Regression

Stages are not a one-way ratchet. A team can drop back when something changes: new members, a new leader, a new or shifted goal, a reorg, or sustained pressure. A Performing team that takes on three new people may return to Forming or Storming. Account for regression rather than assuming a team holds the highest stage it ever reached.

## Pitfalls

The framework fails in predictable ways. Guard against each:

- **Expecting fast linear progression.** Stages take real time and the path is not smooth. Pushing a team to "just perform" skips the work each stage requires.
- **Trying to skip Storming.** Conflict is the stage's necessary work, not a detour around it. A team that never storms has not yet become a team.
- **Mislabeling a conflict-avoidant team as Norming.** A quiet, polite team that never worked through its differences is stalled before Storming, not past it. Surface calm is not cohesion.
- **Ignoring regression.** After a membership, leadership, or goal change, re-diagnose. The previous stage may no longer hold.
- **Applying the wrong leadership style.** Delegating to a Forming team starves it of direction; directing a Performing team smothers it. The style must match the actual stage.
