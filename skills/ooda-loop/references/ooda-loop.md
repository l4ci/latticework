# OODA: the loop, the stages, and the pitfalls

OODA is a continuous decision loop run under time pressure and uncertainty: Observe, Orient, Decide, Act, then back to Observe. John Boyd, a United States Air Force colonel, fighter pilot, and strategist, developed it from the 1970s to the 1990s out of air-combat analysis and his energy-maneuverability theory, and later generalized it well beyond the cockpit.

The loop is not a strictly sequential checklist. Orientation feeds both forward (it shapes how you decide and act) and back (it shapes what you observe next), so the stages overlap and inform each other. A well-oriented party can sometimes skip straight from observation to action without an explicit decision step, acting on trained instinct.

```
        ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
        │                                              │
   Observe ──► Orient ──► Decide ──► Act ──► (result) ─┘
                 ▲ │
                 └─┘   (Orient shapes Observe, Decide, and Act)
```

## The four stages

### Observe
Take in the unfolding situation: raw information, the environment, the other party's actions, and the feedback from your last action. Watch for what has changed and what is surprising. Keep observation separate from interpretation. Naming a fact and explaining it are two different acts, and collapsing them hides the assumptions doing the real work.

### Orient
The schwerpunkt, the focal point, the most important station of the loop. Orientation is how you make sense of the observations: you filter them through your mental models, your prior experience, and your cultural background, and against new information. Boyd described the work as analysis (taking stale models apart) and synthesis (building new ones). Orientation shapes how you observe, what you decide, and how you act, so getting it wrong corrupts everything downstream.

The core skill is destroying and rebuilding your mental model faster than the situation changes. A mismatch between your model and reality, something the model did not predict, is the signal to re-orient. Treat surprise as data, not noise.

### Decide
Choose the next action as a hypothesis to test, given your current orientation. A decision is a bet on what the orientation implies, made fast enough to stay ahead of the situation rather than perfect on paper.

### Act
Carry it out, then feed the result back into Observe. The result, whether it confirms or breaks the orientation, is the most valuable observation of the next loop.

## Key ideas

- **Tempo.** Operating at a faster rhythm than the adversary or the changing situation is the lever. By cycling faster you act on current reality while the other party is still reacting to stale information. This is "getting inside the other party's OODA loop": your moves keep invalidating their orientation before they can finish acting on it.
- **Orientation over speed.** Speed matters, but correct orientation matters more. Fast action on bad orientation loses faster. The aim is to be both quick and well-oriented, not quick at the cost of seeing the situation wrong.
- **Implicit guidance and control.** A team that shares an orientation can act in concert without explicit orders, because each member can predict what the others would do. Shared orientation is what lets a group cycle quickly without a bottleneck at the top.
- **A stance, not a plan.** OODA is a way of operating continuously, not a one-time procedure you complete. The loop never closes; each Act reopens Observe.

## When OODA fits, and when it does not

OODA is for fast-moving, uncertain situations where the situation changes while you deliberate: a live incident, a competitive surprise, a negotiation, a fast-shifting market. Its whole value is keeping a usable model and a fast rhythm when there is no time for a complete analysis.

When you have ample time and a stable problem, the loop's bias toward speed is a liability. A deliberate method that verifies root causes or models the long term will serve better. OODA earns its keep precisely when waiting for certainty would cost more than acting on a good-enough orientation.
