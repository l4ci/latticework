# OODA: Competitor price cut

> _Illustrative example output for the `ooda-loop` skill. Northwind, the competitor "Datapeak", the accounts, and every figure below are fictional and invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. Do not cite these numbers._

- **Situation:** This morning Datapeak, a larger analytics SaaS, cut its mid-tier plan price by 40%. Within two hours our sales team had "match this or we churn" emails from three of our top ten accounts. We are Northwind, a smaller analytics SaaS whose edge is onboarding and support.
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **Context:** Gross margin leaves no room to match a 40% cut without bleeding. We can read churn signals, trial conversions, and support tickets daily. The board meets in two weeks.

---

## Loop 1: The reflex to match

### Observe

- Datapeak cut its mid-tier list price 40%, announced by email and a pricing-page change at ~08:00.
- Three top-ten accounts (Account A, B, C) emailed asking us to match or they walk at renewal.
- **Changed:** the price gap, overnight, from roughly at-parity to Datapeak being 40% cheaper on paper.
- **Surprising:** the cut is on the *mid-tier* plan only, not enterprise, and not the entry plan. The announcement leads with "self-serve" language.

### Orient

- **Model before:** "Datapeak is attacking our whole base on price. If we do not match, we lose accounts across the board. This is a price war and we are out-gunned."
- **Mismatch the observations expose:** a true across-the-board price war would cut every tier, especially enterprise where the revenue is. Datapeak cut *only* mid-tier and wrapped it in self-serve messaging. That is not a war on our base, it looks like a move to fill a self-serve funnel and juice signup numbers, probably ahead of their own funding or growth milestone. The three accounts pushing us are all mid-tier.
- **Model after:** "This is a funnel-growth move aimed at low-touch self-serve customers, not an assault on relationship-heavy accounts. Matching the headline price would torch our margin to defend customers Datapeak is not actually built to serve well."

### Decide

- **Action (hypothesis):** Do *not* match the list price. Instead, call the three at-risk accounts today, before they decide, and reframe the comparison around total cost and onboarding/support rather than sticker price. Expectation: at least two of the three pause their threat once the real comparison is on the table.

### Act and result

- Founder and head of CS called all three within the day.
- **Result:** Account A and C calmed down once shown that Datapeak's cheap tier drops the dedicated onboarding and the SLA they rely on. Account B stayed cold: it turned out B is genuinely price-driven and barely uses our support. One genuinely-at-risk account, two reassured.

---

## Loop 2: The account that did not calm down

### Observe

- Account B still leaning toward Datapeak; its usage logs show low support engagement and heavy use of only the basic dashboards.
- **Changed:** our own read of the threat. It is now one specific account, not three, and that account is a poor fit for our high-touch model.
- **Surprising:** trial signups on *our* site ticked up 15% since the morning, not down. New trials cited Datapeak's announcement, which "put analytics pricing on their radar," then comparison-shopped to us.

### Orient

- **Model before:** "We have three accounts to save and a defensive fight on our hands."
- **Mismatch:** the trial uptick is the tell. Datapeak's noise is *growing the category's attention*, and some of that attention lands on us. We were braced for pure defense; the observations show an opening on offense. And Account B is not a customer our model serves profitably anyway.
- **Model after:** "The real game is not saving every mid-tier account at any cost. It is (a) letting the genuinely price-only, low-touch accounts go without bleeding margin to keep them, and (b) catching the comparison-shoppers Datapeak just stirred up, on the axis where we win: onboarding and support."

### Decide

- **Action (hypothesis):** Stop defending Account B on price; offer it a graceful low-touch downgrade tier at a sustainable price, and if it still leaves, let it. Simultaneously, publish a "switching from Datapeak" onboarding offer aimed at the new trial traffic. Expectation: trial-to-paid conversion rises this week, and the margin we did *not* spend matching list price funds the switching offer.

### Act and result

- Shipped the switching offer to the trial flow within two days. Made Account B a clean downgrade offer.
- **Result:** Account B took the cheaper low-touch tier instead of churning entirely, smaller revenue but positive margin. Trial-to-paid conversion rose from ~18% to ~26% on the week, several citing the onboarding comparison. Net: revenue roughly flat, margin protected, pipeline up.

---

## Tempo note

We got inside Datapeak's loop. Their move assumed competitors would either match (and bleed) or freeze and lose accounts. By re-orienting twice within two days, first seeing the cut as a funnel move rather than a war, then seeing the attention spillover as an opening, we acted on the real situation while they were still executing a one-shot price announcement. The gap is widening in our favor: they made one move, we have completed two loops and are now setting the agenda (the switching offer) rather than reacting to theirs.

The risk to watch: if Datapeak follows with an *enterprise* cut, the "funnel move, not a war" orientation breaks and we re-orient again. That is the loop working, not failing.

---

## Caveats

- **Orientation can be wrong.** The "funnel-growth, not a price war" read fit the observations (mid-tier only, self-serve framing), but it is an inference. An enterprise-tier cut next week would falsify it and force a re-orient. The model is a working hypothesis, not a fact.
- **Speed without correct orientation loses.** The fast reflex here was to match the 40% cut on day one. Acting quickly on the wrong model ("price war") would have destroyed margin to defend customers Datapeak was not even contesting. The second loop's value came from re-orienting, not from moving faster.
- **The loop is a stance, not a one-time plan.** This is not "handle the price cut and done." Datapeak will move again; the board meeting will surface new information. Each Act reopens Observe.
- **When there is time to deliberate, slow down.** The full pricing-strategy question (should mid-tier exist, what is the long-run positioning) is not a fast-loop problem. That deserves a deliberate analysis before the board, not an OODA reflex.
- These figures are illustrative for documentation and were not researched.
