# Value Proposition Canvas: profile and value map

Alexander Osterwalder, Yves Pigneur, Greg Bernarda, and Alan Smith's framework (*Value Proposition Design*, 2014) zooms into two blocks of the Business Model Canvas, Value Propositions and Customer Segments, and tests whether they fit. The point is not to describe an offer and a customer separately but to check that the offer's relievers and creators land on the customer's most important jobs, most severe pains, and most-wanted gains. A value map that addresses pains and gains the customer does not rank highly is a polished answer to the wrong question.

The unit of analysis is a single **value proposition for a single customer segment**. One offer aimed at two segments is two canvases. The customer side is described, never invented: ground it in how the customer actually behaves, then map the value side onto it. Do not fill the value map for a profile you have not grounded.

## The two sides

The canvas has two halves that must be built in order.

- **Customer Profile (right side, the circle).** Describes the customer of the segment. Three parts: Customer Jobs, Pains, Gains. You observe and describe this; you do not design it.
- **Value Map (left side, the square).** Describes the offering. Three parts: Products & Services, Pain Relievers, Gain Creators. You design this to fit the profile.

**Fit** is the match between the two: pain relievers address the customer's most severe pains, gain creators produce their most-wanted gains, and products and services enable their most important jobs.

## Rank importance, do not list

The single discipline that makes this canvas work: rank the customer side before touching the value side. List every job, pain, and gain you can find, then rank each by how much it matters **to the customer**, not to you.

- Jobs: rank by importance (insignificant to important).
- Pains: rank by severity (moderate to extreme).
- Gains: rank by relevance (nice-to-have to essential).

An unranked profile is a checklist. A ranked profile tells you which few items the value map must hit, and exposes relievers and creators aimed at items nobody cares about.

## Facts versus assumptions

Every item on both sides is either observed or believed. Mark each material claim a **fact** (evidence: observed behavior, interview data, real usage, sales records) or an **assumption** (a hypothesis still to test), and rate confidence **high, medium, or low**. The riskiest items are usually on the customer side, where assuming a job, pain, or gain matters more than it does sends the whole value map chasing a phantom. Treating an assumption as a fact is the most common way this canvas misleads.

## Customer Profile

Describe the customer. Do not invent the customer to fit the offer.

### Customer Jobs

What the customer is trying to get done in their work and life, in their own words. Three kinds plus a supporting role:

- **Functional jobs**: a specific task or problem to solve (write a report, eat healthily, get to work).
- **Social jobs**: how the customer wants to be perceived (look competent, stay on trend, gain status).
- **Emotional jobs**: a state the customer seeks (feel secure, feel good about a choice, avoid anxiety).
- **Supporting jobs**: roles the customer plays around the main job, as buyer of value (compare offers, decide, pay), co-creator of value (give feedback, contribute), or transferrer of value (dispose of, resell, pass on).

Rank jobs by importance to the customer. Note the context, which jobs are frequent, and which the customer struggles to complete.

### Pains

Bad outcomes, obstacles, and risks the customer experiences before, during, or after trying to get a job done.

- **Undesired outcomes, problems, and characteristics**: functional (a solution that does not work), social (looks bad doing it), emotional (feels frustrated), or ancillary (annoying to find or buy).
- **Obstacles**: what stops the customer from even starting, or slows them down.
- **Risks**: what could go wrong, and how badly.

Rank pains by severity, from extreme to moderate. Note how often each pain occurs.

### Gains

Outcomes and benefits the customer wants. Four kinds, by expectation:

- **Required gains**: without them the solution does not work (a phone must make calls).
- **Expected gains**: basic expectations even if not strictly required (a phone should be well designed).
- **Desired gains**: gains the customer wishes for and would name if asked (a phone integrates with my other devices).
- **Unexpected gains**: gains beyond expectations the customer would not think to ask for (the touchscreen interface before anyone expected one).

Rank gains by relevance to the customer, from essential to nice-to-have.

## Value Map

Describe the offering. Build this after the profile is grounded and ranked, mapping each item onto specific profile items.

### Products & Services

The list of what the value proposition is built on: everything the customer can buy or use. Physical/tangible, intangible (rights, services), digital (downloads, recommendations), or financial (funds, insurance). These create value only by helping with a job, so each should connect to a customer job. Not every product matters equally; note which are essential to the proposition.

### Pain Relievers

How the products and services kill or reduce specific customer pains. For each reliever, name the exact pain it addresses and how (eliminates it, reduces it, caps the risk). The best relievers target the most severe pains; relievers aimed at moderate pains add little. A reliever with no pain on the profile to point to does not belong on the map.

### Gain Creators

How the products and services produce specific customer gains. For each creator, name the exact gain it produces and how (delivers a required gain, exceeds an expected one, creates a desired or unexpected one). The best creators produce the most-wanted gains; a creator that produces a gain nobody ranks highly is wasted effort.

## Fit

The value map fits the profile when its relievers and creators land on the customer's most important items, not on a long tail of minor ones. Check item by item.

| Customer side (ranked) | Value side | Fit? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Top jobs (most important) | Products & services that enable them | Hit / partial / miss |
| Top pains (most severe) | Pain relievers that address them | Hit / partial / miss |
| Top gains (most wanted) | Gain creators that produce them | Hit / partial / miss |

Two failures to flag in both directions:

- **Unaddressed top items.** A high-ranked job, pain, or gain with no product, reliever, or creator pointing at it. This is the gap to close.
- **Irrelevant relievers and creators.** A reliever or creator that addresses a low-ranked or absent pain/gain. This is effort spent where it does not move the customer. Cut it or justify it.

Good fit is not addressing everything. It is addressing the few things the customer ranks highest, and not wasting the map on the rest.

## Three levels of fit

Fit is achieved at three escalating levels, each needing harder evidence:

- **Problem-solution fit (the canvas).** On paper, the value map addresses the profile's important jobs, severe pains, and wanted gains. Established with the canvas and reasoning. **This is what this skill targets.**
- **Product-market fit (the market).** The value proposition actually creates value, evidenced by real customer behavior in the market, not just on the canvas. Needs market evidence the canvas alone cannot give.
- **Business-model fit (the BMC).** The value proposition can be embedded in a profitable, scalable business model. Established on the Business Model Canvas, where viability is tested.

A canvas can show problem-solution fit and still fail the next two levels. State which level the analysis reaches and do not claim more.

## Common pitfalls

- **Filling the value map for an ungrounded profile.** Building relievers and creators against a customer you imagined rather than observed. Ground and rank the profile first.
- **Listing instead of ranking.** Three full lists with no order. Without ranking by importance to the customer, fit cannot be judged, because every item looks equal.
- **Relievers and creators for low-ranked items.** The classic failure: effort poured into pains and gains the customer does not care much about, while the top items go unaddressed. Always map the value side onto the ranked customer side, not onto a generic wish list.
- **Confusing your priorities with the customer's.** Ranking by what the team finds interesting or easy to build, not by what matters to the customer. The ranking is the customer's, evidenced where possible.
- **Claiming product-market fit from the canvas.** The canvas can show problem-solution fit. Calling that product-market fit skips the market evidence the higher level requires.
