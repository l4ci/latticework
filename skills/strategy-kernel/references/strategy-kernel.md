# Strategy Kernel: the diagnosis, guiding policy, and coherent action

From Richard Rumelt, *Good Strategy / Bad Strategy* (2011). A real strategy is not a vision, a goal, or a list of priorities. It is a way of dealing with a specific challenge, and it has a hard core Rumelt calls the **kernel**: three connected parts that must hold together.

## The kernel: three parts

### 1. Diagnosis

The diagnosis defines and explains the nature of the challenge. It simplifies the overwhelming complexity of a situation into a crux, naming what is really going on. A situation has a thousand facts; the diagnosis says which one or two are doing the work. A good diagnosis often replaces the noise with a metaphor or a single dominant fact, and that act of simplification is itself a judgment about what matters.

A diagnosis can be wrong, which is exactly what makes it useful: a strategy built on a stated diagnosis can be argued with, tested, and corrected. A strategy with no diagnosis cannot be evaluated at all.

### 2. Guiding policy

The guiding policy is the overall approach chosen to cope with or overcome the obstacle the diagnosis identifies. It is directional. It is a signpost that points the way and a guardrail that keeps action on the road, not a goal and not a list of actions. A guiding policy creates advantage by anticipating the actions and reactions of others, by reducing the complexity the diagnosis named, and by concentrating effort.

The test of a guiding policy is that it **rules things out**. If it is compatible with every possible action, it is steering nothing. A guiding policy that forecloses options is doing its job.

### 3. Coherent action

Coherent action is the set of coordinated steps, resource commitments, and policies that carry out the guiding policy. The word that matters is **coherent**: the actions must reinforce one another rather than fight each other. Two sensible actions that pull in opposite directions cancel out. The power of a strategy comes from the coordination, the way each commitment makes the others more effective, not from any single move.

## The crux

The crux is the part of the challenge that is both **most important and addressable**, the point where focused effort actually pays off. Many challenges are hard because they are tangled; the crux is the knot that, once cut, lets the rest fall away. Good strategy finds the crux and points the guiding policy straight at it. Spreading effort evenly across every part of a problem is the opposite of strategy.

## Sources of power

Good strategy concentrates resources on a decisive point rather than spreading them thin. Rumelt names several sources of leverage a guiding policy can draw on:

- **Focus / concentration:** apply effort to a single objective where it tips the balance, instead of diluting it across many.
- **Anticipation:** act on the predictable behavior of customers, competitors, or the market before they do.
- **Pivot point:** find the place where a small applied force releases a large stored effect.
- **Chain of effect:** order moves so each one sets up the next, building momentum.
- **Coherent design:** coordinate the pieces so the whole is stronger than the sum, like a well-designed machine.

A strategy that says yes to everything has no power, because it concentrates nothing.

## Bad-strategy detector: the four hallmarks

Rumelt names four hallmarks of bad strategy. Any one of them is a warning that what is on the page is not a strategy. Run the result against all four.

**(a) Fluff.** Gluey abstraction and inflated jargon dressed up as insight. A sentence that sounds profound but says nothing once you strip the buzzwords ("our strategy is to be a customer-centric, synergistic platform leader"). Fluff hides the absence of thought.

**(b) Failure to face the challenge.** No diagnosis, so the challenge is never named. A strategy that does not define the obstacle cannot be evaluated, argued with, or improved, because there is nothing to check it against. This is the most damaging hallmark.

**(c) Mistaking goals for strategy.** Statements of desire standing in for a method. "Grow revenue 20%" or "become the market leader" describe a destination, not a route. A goal says where to arrive; a strategy says how to get there given the obstacle in the way.

**(d) Bad strategic objectives.** The objectives that translate the guiding policy into action are themselves broken. Two common forms:
- **Scramble:** a long dog's-dinner list of things to do, every department's wish bolted on, with no concentration and no logic of which few matter. A list is not a strategy.
- **Blue-sky objectives:** objectives that simply restate the challenge as a goal ("the objective is to close the performance gap") without any account of how. They skip the work and rename the problem as the answer.

## How to use the kernel

1. **Write the diagnosis first.** Name the crux: the one obstacle that, addressed, unlocks the rest. Resist listing every difficulty; pick the dominant fact.
2. **Set a guiding policy that rules things out.** Make a hard choice, lean on a named source of power, and state explicitly what the policy declines to do.
3. **Design coherent action.** A handful of coordinated commitments, each tied to the guiding policy, checked so they reinforce rather than conflict.
4. **Audit against the four hallmarks.** Fluff, failure to face the challenge, goals-as-strategy, bad objectives. Fix whatever fails before calling the kernel done.

A kernel is a hypothesis, not a guarantee. It states a bet on what matters and how to win, in a form precise enough to be wrong.
