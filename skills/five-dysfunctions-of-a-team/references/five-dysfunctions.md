# The Five Dysfunctions of a Team: the pyramid, the tells, and the interventions

Patrick Lencioni's model (*The Five Dysfunctions of a Team*, 2002) is a hierarchy, not a checklist. The five dysfunctions stack into a pyramid, and each one rests on the one below it. A team cannot fix a higher layer while the layer beneath it is broken: the higher symptom is usually the lower break showing through.

```
                    /\
                   /  \   5. Inattention to Results
                  /----\
                 /      \  4. Avoidance of Accountability
                /--------\
               /          \ 3. Lack of Commitment
              /------------\
             /              \ 2. Fear of Conflict
            /----------------\
           /                  \ 1. Absence of Trust   (the base)
          /--------------------\
```

## The hierarchy rule

Read the pyramid bottom to top. Each layer is the precondition for the one above:

- **Conflict requires trust.** People who do not trust each other will not risk open disagreement.
- **Commitment requires conflict.** People who have not aired their views and been heard will not truly buy in.
- **Accountability requires commitment.** People who never committed to a clear plan have no shared standard to hold each other to.
- **Results requires accountability.** People who do not hold each other to the standard drift back to individual goals and ego.

The consequence: a cracked base surfaces as symptoms higher up. A team that "can't seem to commit" often does not actually have a commitment problem; it has a conflict problem (decisions made without real debate) or a trust problem (people withholding) two layers down. Diagnosing the visible symptom and intervening there fails, because the foundation it needs is missing.

## The five layers, bottom to top

### 1. Absence of Trust (the base)

**Definition.** The absence of *vulnerability-based* trust: the willingness to be genuinely open with one another about mistakes, weaknesses, and needs. Not predictive trust ("I can predict what they'll do"), but the confidence that one's exposed flank will not be used against them.

**Tells / symptoms.** Members hide their weaknesses and mistakes from one another; conceal failures; refuse to ask for help or offer help outside their own area; hesitate to give feedback; jump to conclusions about others' intentions; hold grudges; guard their reputations; dread spending time together.

**Interventions.** Personal-histories exercise; team effectiveness exercise (each member names one peer's biggest contribution and one area to improve); personality and behavioral profiling (e.g., MBTI, DiSC) to build understanding; 360-degree feedback used developmentally, not for evaluation; the leader models vulnerability first by admitting their own weaknesses and mistakes.

### 2. Fear of Conflict

**Definition.** The pursuit of artificial harmony over productive, ideological conflict. Teams that lack trust cannot engage in unfiltered, passionate debate about ideas, so they paper over disagreement.

**Tells / symptoms.** Boring, guarded meetings; no productive debate; ideas not challenged; back-channel politics and personal attacks that surface when issues are not aired openly; important topics avoided; the same issues raised again and again because they are never resolved.

**Interventions.** Mining for buried disagreements and forcing them into the open; real-time permission ("this is good, keep pushing") during a hard exchange so people learn conflict is productive, not destructive; assigning a member to play devil's advocate or surface the contrarian view; the leader restraining the urge to protect people from harm and instead demonstrating that conflict about ideas is acceptable.

### 3. Lack of Commitment

**Definition.** The failure to achieve genuine buy-in to decisions, producing ambiguity about direction and priorities. Commitment depends on clarity and on having been heard, not on consensus.

**Tells / symptoms.** Ambiguity about direction and priorities; decisions revisited endlessly; windows of opportunity missed by over-analysis and delay; fear of failure that drives endless deliberation; fence-sitting; lack of confidence and second-guessing among members.

**Interventions.** At the end of every meeting, explicitly review and cascade the decisions agreed and what will be communicated; set hard deadlines and honor them; run contingency and worst-case scenarios to lower the fear of being wrong; force decisions on ambiguous matters to show that a clear decision is better than none. The principle of *disagree and commit*: a heard team member commits even without full agreement.

### 4. Avoidance of Accountability

**Definition.** The reluctance of peers to call out one another's behaviors and performance that hurt the team. Accountability here means peer-to-peer, not just leader-to-report.

**Tells / symptoms.** Resentment among members who hold different standards; mediocrity tolerated; missed deadlines and deliverables; the leader carrying the entire burden of discipline as the sole source of accountability; peers unwilling to confront one another on underperformance or unproductive behavior.

**Interventions.** Publish goals and standards so expectations are explicit and there is no excuse for inaction; regular, lightweight progress reviews where peers discuss performance against the published standard; team rewards rather than purely individual ones, so peers have a stake in each other's output; the leader serving as the ultimate arbiter only when the peer mechanism fails, not as the first resort.

### 5. Inattention to Results

**Definition.** The tendency to put individual status, ego, and departmental goals above the collective results of the team. The top of the pyramid and the ultimate point of the whole structure.

**Tells / symptoms.** The team stagnates and fails to grow; loses achievement-oriented members; rarely beats competitors; members focused on personal career, recognition, or their own department's goals over the team's; silos; the collective scoreboard ignored.

**Interventions.** Keep results public via a visible, shared scoreboard so the team's collective outcomes are unambiguous; declare the desired results out loud and tie rewards to achieving them; the leader staying selfless and objective, reserving recognition and reward for those who contribute to the team's goals.

## How to diagnose

1. Gather **behavioral evidence**, not impressions. Each layer is defined by observable tells; score against the tells, cite what was actually seen or said.
2. Assess **every layer**, even ones that look healthy, because a higher layer can look broken only because a lower one is.
3. Find the **lowest broken layer**. That is the binding constraint. Everything above it inherits the break.
4. Prescribe **starting at the base** and rebuilding **upward**: there is no point installing accountability rituals on a team that cannot yet engage in conflict.

## Classic failure modes (guard against these)

- **Treating the five as independent checkboxes.** They are a hierarchy. Scoring each in isolation and summing them misses the entire model.
- **Chasing the surface symptom.** "We need more accountability" when the real break is absence of trust two layers down. The accountability fix will not take, because its preconditions are missing. Always trace a high-layer symptom down to see whether a lower layer is feeding it.
- **Diagnosing without evidence.** Asserting "low trust" because the team feels off. Each verdict must rest on specific tells observed.
- **Starting the rebuild at the top.** Sequencing interventions out of order. Trust first, then conflict, then commitment, then accountability, then results.
