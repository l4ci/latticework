# okr

## What this is

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) turn strategy into measurable goals. The method came out of Intel under Andy Grove in the 1970s, traveled to Google with John Doerr, and reached a wide audience through his book *Measure What Matters* (2018), which is where most teams pick it up. The idea is a pairing: a qualitative objective says what you want to achieve and why it matters, and 3-5 quantitative key results prove it, so that hitting every key result means the objective is met by definition. That completeness rule is the test of a set, and the outcome-versus-output distinction is what keeps a key result from collapsing into a task list.

The anatomy of objectives and key results, scoring, cadence, alignment, and the pitfalls live in [references/okr.md](references/okr.md).

## What it does

The skill facilitates one drafting cycle. It clarifies the objective as a direction-setting sentence with the metrics stripped out, drafts the key results with numbers and baselines, then stress-tests them with a small set of critics running in parallel: one checking whether each key result is an outcome rather than a task, one whether it is actually measurable, one whether the set fully covers the objective, and one whether the ambition is sandbagged or impossible. After the rewrite it sets the ambition level, cadence, owners, and scoring, and checks how the OKRs ladder up to higher-level goals. The output is a saved OKR document with a check log.

The critics exist because of where OKRs usually go wrong: key results drift into task lists that can all be done while the objective still fails, objectives turn into vanity statements, and targets get sandbagged so every score lands at 1.0. The skill names committed versus aspirational OKRs so a 0.7 reads correctly, and it keeps scoring out of compensation, since tying the two together pushes teams to sandbag.

## When to use it

Reach for OKRs when you want to convert a strategy or ambition into goals that can be measured and reviewed, usually for a quarter. The question OKRs answer is "what are we committing to achieve this period, and how will we know."

- For the broader, durable performance-measurement frame that links goals across financial, customer, process, and learning perspectives, use [balanced-scorecard](../balanced-scorecard/); OKRs are the goal-setting layer that cycles within it.
- For the heavier strategy-deployment lineage with an X-matrix and formal catchball across levels, use [hoshin-kanri](../hoshin-kanri/).
- For scoring and ranking a backlog of features rather than setting period goals, use [rice-prioritization](../rice-prioritization/).
