# Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument: the two axes and five modes

The Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument (TKI), developed by Kenneth Thomas and Ralph Kilmann, places conflict-handling behavior on two axes:

- **Assertiveness**: the degree to which you try to satisfy your own concerns.
- **Cooperativeness**: the degree to which you try to satisfy the other party's concerns.

| | Uncooperative | Cooperative |
|---|---|---|
| **Assertive** | Competing | Collaborating |
| **Unassertive** | Avoiding | Accommodating |

**Compromising** sits in the center, intermediate on both axes.

The instrument's foundational claim: no mode is best. Each is effective in some situations and ineffective in others. The skill of conflict handling is matching the mode to the situation in front of you, not defaulting to the one you reach for by habit.

## The situational factors

Before scoring any mode, fix these facts about THIS conflict. They decide which modes fit:

- **The stakes for you**: how important is the outcome to your own goals.
- **The stakes for them**: how important is the outcome to the other party.
- **The relationship**: how much the ongoing relationship matters versus this single outcome.
- **Power balance**: relative authority, leverage, and the realistic chance of prevailing.
- **Time pressure**: whether a fast resolution is needed or there is room to work the problem.
- **Trust and history**: whether the other party negotiates in good faith.

## The five modes

### Competing (assertive, uncooperative)
Win/lose. You pursue your own concerns at the other party's expense, using whatever power you have.
- **Fits**: quick, decisive action is needed (emergencies); a vital but unpopular call must be made; defending against someone exploiting non-competitive behavior; an issue where you know you are right and the cost of losing is high.
- **Overuse risk**: damages relationships, surrounds you with yes-people, suppresses input you needed; others stop bringing you problems.
- **Underuse risk**: you feel powerless, cannot act when action is required, withhold a stand even when you are right.

### Collaborating (assertive, cooperative)
Win/win. You work with the other party to find a solution that fully satisfies both concerns.
- **Fits**: both sets of concerns are too important to be compromised; you need genuine buy-in and commitment; you want to merge insights from different perspectives; the relationship warrants the investment.
- **Overuse risk**: spends time and energy on trivial issues that do not deserve it; can be a way to avoid deciding; invites others to exploit the openness.
- **Underuse risk**: you miss the joint gains that only emerge when both parties' full concerns are on the table; relationships stay shallow.

### Compromising (intermediate on both)
You find a middle ground that partially satisfies both. Each gives up something.
- **Fits**: goals are moderately important but not worth the effort of full collaboration; two parties of roughly equal power are committed to opposing positions; time pressure forces an expedient settlement; a temporary fix or a fallback when collaboration or competing fails.
- **Overuse risk**: nobody is fully satisfied; you split the difference reflexively and shortcut a better solution that collaboration would have found; principle gets traded away for expedience.
- **Underuse risk**: too much haggling over small things; you cannot make the practical concessions that get a deal done.

### Avoiding (unassertive, uncooperative)
You sidestep the conflict: postpone it, withdraw, or do not raise it.
- **Fits**: the issue is trivial or a symptom of a bigger one; you have no chance of satisfying your concerns; the cost of confronting outweighs the benefit; people need to cool down and regain perspective; someone else can resolve it more effectively; more information is needed before acting.
- **Overuse risk**: issues fester, decisions drift by default, coordination suffers; people walk on eggshells around the unaddressed conflict.
- **Underuse risk**: you make enemies by confronting everything; you cannot prioritize and get drawn into every dispute.

### Accommodating (unassertive, cooperative)
You yield. You set aside your own concerns to satisfy the other party's.
- **Fits**: you realize you are wrong or the other position is better; the issue matters far more to them than to you; you are building social credit for later issues that matter more; preserving harmony and stability outweighs the outcome; you are outmatched and continuing would only hurt you; letting subordinates learn from their own mistakes.
- **Overuse risk**: your own needs and ideas get neglected; your influence erodes; it signals you can be rolled; you lose track of what you actually need.
- **Underuse risk**: you cannot yield even when you are wrong or it costs you little; you struggle to build goodwill or recognize when someone else simply needs the win.

## How to read the diagnosis

The right mode falls out of the situational factors, not from a personality type:

- **High stakes for you, low for them, time-critical** → Competing.
- **High stakes for both, relationship and buy-in matter, time available** → Collaborating.
- **Moderate stakes both sides, equal power, time-limited** → Compromising.
- **Low stakes, no path to winning, or needs cooling off** → Avoiding.
- **Low stakes for you, high for them, relationship matters** → Accommodating.

Most situations admit a primary mode and a fallback if it fails (collaboration that stalls often falls back to compromising; competing against bad faith may fall back to avoiding).

## Pitfalls the instrument is built to catch

- **No mode is universally best.** Chronic collaborating burns time on trivia; chronic avoiding lets issues rot; chronic competing hollows out relationships. The failure is a single default applied to every situation.
- **Ignoring power and time.** A collaborative ideal is useless when you have no leverage or no time. Read the real constraints before choosing.
- **Labeling people, not situations.** The TKI describes behavior in a situation, not a fixed personality. Use it to choose an approach for THIS conflict, not to box someone in.
- **Confusing compromise with collaboration.** Splitting the difference is not the same as satisfying both concerns fully. Reach for compromise when the issue does not justify collaboration, not as a reflex.
