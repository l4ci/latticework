# First principles: method, bedrock test, and reconstruction

First-principles thinking reasons up from fundamental truths rather than across from how a problem is already solved. The two ways of reasoning are worth naming because the method is a deliberate choice between them:

- **Reasoning by analogy** copies a known solution: we do it this way because that is how it is done, because that is what a competitor does, because that is what the form has always looked like. It is fast and usually right, which is why it dominates.
- **Reasoning from first principles** ignores the existing solution and builds up from the few things that genuinely hold: physical limits, definitions, established facts, hard constraints, and the real goal. It is slow and expensive, and it is the only way to find an option the inherited form has been hiding.

A first principle, in Aristotle, is a foundational proposition that cannot be deduced from any other: the bedrock a chain of reasoning bottoms out in. The whole method is the discipline of digging down to that bedrock and refusing to stop at a convention dressed up as a fact.

## The core distinction: fact versus assumption

Every claim about a problem is one of two things, and the method lives or dies on telling them apart:

- A **fact** (a genuine first principle) is a truth you cannot reduce further or can verify empirically: a physical or material limit, a definition, an established figure, a statute, the real requirement. "Aluminium has a market price of roughly X per kilogram." "A human needs to physically reach the location to swap the hardware."
- An **assumption** is a judgment inherited by analogy and taken as fixed because that is how it has always been done: "support means a desk staffed around the clock," "a battery pack must cost what battery packs cost today," "the device has to be sold through retail." It feels like a constraint but is really a custom.

The classic illustration is cost. Asked what a battery pack must cost, reasoning by analogy answers "about what they have always cost, they are expensive." Reasoning from first principles asks what a battery pack *is*: cobalt, nickel, aluminium, carbon, some polymers, a steel can. Price those raw materials on the spot market, add the cost of combining them, and you get a floor that can sit far below the inherited price. The inherited price was an assumption; the material cost is closer to bedrock.

## Procedure

1. **State the problem and the conventional approach.** Name the problem, separate from any solution. Name the received or current way it is solved, the "we do it this way because that is how it is done" answer, and name the goal that approach is actually meant to serve. Keep the goal separate from its current form: "move people across the city" is the goal, "run more buses" is one form of it.

2. **Surface and challenge the assumptions.** List the things the conventional approach takes as fixed. For each, ask the one question that drives the whole method: is this necessarily true, or merely customary and inherited by analogy? Treat every item as inherited until a test proves otherwise. The default posture is to challenge, not to accept.

3. **Decompose to bedrock.** Strip away the assumptions that do not survive until you reach the fundamental truths that genuinely hold: physical limits, definitions, established facts, hard constraints, and the real goal. A bedrock truth is one you cannot reduce further or can verify empirically. As you go, separate fact from assumption and flag each, because a buried assumption mistaken for a fact will poison everything built on it.

4. **Reconstruct upward.** Build a solution using only the bedrock truths and the real goal, ignoring inherited form. Ask: given only these fundamentals, what is actually possible? This is where the hidden option appears, the one the conventional shape made invisible. Do not adjust the convention at the edges; rebuild from the foundation.

5. **Contrast with convention.** Set the reconstructed answer beside the conventional one. Show what convention was costing, which assumption that cost rested on, and what new options open up once the assumption is dropped. If the convention survives the contrast intact, that too is a result: it means the received approach is load-bearing, not merely inherited.

## The bedrock test

A claim earns the label "bedrock" only if it passes one of these:

- **Irreducible.** It cannot be broken into more basic truths (a definition, a logical necessity, the real goal stated plainly).
- **Empirically verifiable.** It can be checked against the world (a measured material cost, a physical limit, a published statute, an established figure).

Anything that fails both is an assumption, however confident it feels. Flag it as such and carry it forward as a judgment, not a foundation.

## Common failure modes

- **Reasoning rigorously from a wrong "fundamental."** The logic above a false bedrock is impeccable and the conclusion is still wrong. Verify the bedrock; do not assume it. This is why every fundamental is flagged fact or assumption.
- **Stopping too early.** Halting at a convention dressed up as a constraint ("the battery just costs that"). If a claim can be reduced further, it is not yet bedrock.
- **Mistaking the form for the goal.** Reconstructing "a better bus schedule" when the goal was "move people," and so missing the option outside the inherited form.
- **Using it everywhere.** First-principles reasoning is expensive. For routine problems where analogy works fine, it is wasted effort. The method earns its cost only when the convention is suspect, costly, or untested.
- **Treating an option as proven.** The method surfaces what the bedrock allows. It does not prove that option feasible, costed, staffed, or legal. A reconstruction is a candidate, not a verdict.

## Source

First-principles thinking sits in the mental-models tradition: Charlie Munger's latticework of models and the Farnam Street writing that popularized it, reaching back to Aristotle's notion of a first principle (*archē*) as a foundational proposition that cannot be deduced from any other. It is the reasoning method behind cost-from-raw-materials decompositions and any analysis that refuses to take the inherited answer as given.
