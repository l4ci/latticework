# self-determination-theory

## What this is

Self-Determination Theory (SDT) is a theory of human motivation from Edward Deci and Richard Ryan at the University of Rochester, formalized in 1985, stated canonically by Ryan and Deci in 2000, and revisited in their 2020 retrospective. Its Basic Psychological Needs Theory holds that three innate needs drive motivation, performance, and wellbeing when they are met and drive disengagement and ill-being when they are thwarted: Autonomy (acting from one's own volition and values), Competence (feeling effective and growing), and Relatedness (belonging and mattering to others). Two distinctions do the work. First, each need is read twice, for satisfaction and for frustration, because active thwarting does more damage than mere low satisfaction. Second, motivation is treated as a matter of quality, not amount: a continuum that runs from controlled (rewards, punishments, guilt) to autonomous (personal value, identity, inherent interest), with need satisfaction the force that moves motivation toward the autonomous end. Daniel Pink's *Drive* (autonomy, mastery, purpose) is a popular restatement of it; this skill follows the validated theory.

The three needs, the satisfaction-versus-frustration distinction, the motivation continuum, and the diagnostic prompts live in [references/self-determination-theory.md](references/self-determination-theory.md).

## What it does

The skill runs the diagnosis in parallel. Three analyst subagents each take one need (Autonomy, Competence, Relatedness), score both its satisfaction and its frustration in the situation, ground the read in evidence, and judge how much it bears on the observed signs. A synthesis pass then names the binding need (the most thwarted, least satisfied one driving the problem), places the subject's current motivation on the controlled-to-autonomous continuum, and prescribes targeted need-supportive moves for each deficient need. The output is a saved markdown report with a need profile table, the three need reads, the binding need, the motivation-quality read, the interventions, and a re-check.

One rule keeps it honest and guards against the framework's classic failure: disengagement is treated as a specific need being thwarted, not as a generic motivation deficit to be papered over with a reward or a pep talk. The report names which need is the wound and warns against the mislabel.

## When to use it

Reach for SDT when a person, role, or team has gone disengaged, drifting, resistant, or burnt out and you want to know what is actually driving it and what would restore it, including when the motivation in question is your own. The unit is one person or one bounded group in one domain, read for the three needs.

- If the question is the mechanics of one specific behaviour (capability, opportunity, motivation), use [com-b](../com-b/).
- If the question is where a specific change or message will land as a social threat, use [scarf](../scarf/).
- If the question is the standing climate of a team rather than one person's motivation, use [psychological-safety](../psychological-safety/).
- If motivation is understood and the task is to set goals, use [okr](../okr/).

## How to get started

**You provide:** whose motivation is in question, the domain it concerns, and the signs that prompted the question, plus any context on recent changes and what has been tried. Something like "A senior engineer on my team has quietly checked out since the reorg, work is technical delivery, and I want to know why and how to bring them back." If you are diagnosing your own motivation, say so and the skill can interview you.
