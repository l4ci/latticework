# Self-Determination Theory: Checked-Out Senior Engineer

> _Illustrative example output for the `self-determination-theory` skill. Sam, the reorg, and every detail below are fictional, invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. SDT diagnoses the sources of motivation; it is not a clinical assessment. Do not cite this as real data._

- **Subject:** Sam, a strong senior backend engineer, four years on the team, until recently one of the most self-driven people on it.
- **Domain:** Day-to-day engineering work on the payments platform: design, delivery, and code review.
- **Observed signs:** Since the reorg three months ago, Sam does the minimum, has stopped volunteering for hard problems, is terse in review, skips optional design discussions, and the spark is visibly gone. No drop in code quality yet.
- **Context:** The reorg introduced a central architecture council whose sign-off is now required before any non-trivial change ships. Sam used to make those calls directly. Same team, same pay, same well-liked peers.
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **Note:** SDT reads the sources and quality of motivation, not a clinical or psychiatric state. The ratings below are analyst judgment from self-report and inference, not measurement.

---

## Need profile

Each need is read twice, as two distinct measures. Active **frustration** (thwarting) is weighted more heavily than mere low **satisfaction**, because a thwarted need does more damage than a neglected one.

| Need | Satisfaction | Frustration | Bearing on the problem |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Autonomy** | **Low** | **High** | **High** |
| Competence | High | Low | Low |
| Relatedness | Medium-High | Low | Low |

**Binding need: Autonomy.** Competence and relatedness are largely intact. The disengagement traces almost entirely to autonomy being actively thwarted by the new approval gate.

---

## Need detail

### Autonomy: volition and ownership

- **Satisfaction (Low).** Sam no longer experiences their work as self-directed. The decisions that used to be theirs (how to design a change, when it is ready to ship) now route through a council. The sense of ownership that drove four years of strong work has been removed at its source.
- **Frustration (High).** This is active thwarting, not mere neglect. A discretion Sam *used to have* was taken away, which is sharper than never having had it. Every non-trivial change is now a request for permission from people outside the daily work, with the implicit message that Sam's judgment is no longer trusted to ship. The terseness in review and the withdrawal from optional design discussions are textbook autonomy-thwarting responses: when volition is removed, people stop investing discretionary effort and retreat to the minimum the rules require.
- **Tells:** Stopped volunteering for hard problems (no point owning what you cannot decide), skips optional design forums (input without authority feels pointless), does exactly what is asked and no more (the signature of externally imposed control).
- **Supports here:** Genuinely none, currently. The gate is the dominant feature of the new environment.
- **Thwarts here:** The mandatory council sign-off on changes Sam used to own, imposed top-down, applied uniformly regardless of the change's risk, with no rationale given that Sam found persuasive.
- **Confidence:** High. The timing (onset at the reorg), the specificity of what changed (decision rights, nothing else), and the classic withdrawal pattern all converge.

### Competence: effectiveness and growth

- **Satisfaction (High).** Sam is still a strong engineer doing work well within their capability, and code quality has not dropped. Nothing about the reorg reduced their skill or the difficulty of the technical work itself.
- **Frustration (Low).** The need is not being thwarted. The architecture gate is a control on autonomy, not a judgment that Sam is incompetent, and it has not been framed or experienced as performance criticism. There is no evidence of feeling ineffective.
- **Tells:** Quality holds; the issue is willingness, not ability. A competence wound would look like avoidance of hard work for fear of failing; Sam avoids hard work because owning it is no longer rewarding, which is a different signature.
- **Supports here:** A genuinely capable engineer on familiar, well-matched technical problems.
- **Thwarts here:** Watch for a secondary risk: if the gate keeps blocking Sam's calls and shipping their work slows, the felt sense of effectiveness can erode over time. Not present now, but on the horizon.
- **Confidence:** High that competence is not the binding need; medium on the forward risk.

### Relatedness: belonging and mattering

- **Satisfaction (Medium-High).** Same team, same peers, who still like and respect Sam. The interpersonal fabric is intact; Sam has not been isolated or excluded by the people they work with daily.
- **Frustration (Low).** Not actively thwarted by the immediate team. There is a mild, indirect strain: the council is a faceless out-group body now sitting over Sam's work, and being overruled by strangers can chip at the sense of mattering. But it reads as a distant secondary effect, not the wound.
- **Tells:** Terseness in review is better explained by autonomy withdrawal (disengagement from a process that no longer feels like theirs) than by any breakdown in belonging; relationships outside review appear unchanged.
- **Supports here:** A respected, valued position among peers who like working with them.
- **Thwarts here:** Only the faint distance introduced by an external body making calls over Sam's work.
- **Confidence:** Medium-high. The team relationships are clearly fine; the only uncertainty is how much the council's remoteness erodes mattering over time.

---

## Binding need

**Autonomy is the binding need, on its own.** It is the only need with high frustration, and its bearing on the observed signs is high. Competence and relatedness are both in good shape, which is exactly why the diagnosis is clean: a strong, well-liked engineer who has gone quiet, with skill and belonging intact, is almost always an autonomy story. The single environmental change that coincides with the onset (an approval gate that stripped decision rights Sam used to hold) is a direct, specific autonomy thwart.

**The mislabeling trap.** The obvious reading of "Sam has checked out" is *Sam has lost motivation* or *Sam needs re-motivating*, and the reflex fix is a reward, a stretch project dangled as a carrot, a motivational conversation, or a performance nudge. Every one of those misreads the problem and several make it worse. Sam is not short on motivation in the abstract; a specific need, autonomy, is being actively thwarted. Adding an external reward to a person whose autonomy is being crushed pushes their motivation further toward the controlled end, the opposite of what is needed. Dangling a hard project while the council still gates Sam's decisions offers ownership with one hand and removes it with the other. The wound is decision rights; only restoring some real ownership treats it.

---

## Motivation quality

Before the reorg, Sam sat near the **intrinsic / integrated** end of the continuum: they took on hard problems because the work itself was engaging and being a strong engineer was part of who they are. That is why they were one of the most driven people on the team, no external push required.

The autonomy thwart has dragged their motivation toward the **external-regulation** end. Sam now does what the rules require and no more, which is the behavioural signature of controlled motivation: effort contingent on external demand rather than internal endorsement. The danger is the trajectory, not just the current position. Left alone, externally regulated motivation tends to decay toward **amotivation** (going through the motions, then leaving). The drop in discretionary effort is the leading indicator; the lagging one, if nothing changes, is attrition. The aim of intervention is not "more motivation" but moving Sam back toward the autonomous end by restoring ownership.

---

## Interventions

Lift the thwarting first, then rebuild ownership. The binding need is autonomy, so every primary move restores real decision rights, not the feeling of them.

**Autonomy (the binding need, address first).**
- *Owner: Sam's manager, with the architecture council.* Right-size the gate. Most teams that add a blanket approval council do not actually intend to review trivial and low-risk changes. Define a risk threshold (blast radius, data sensitivity, reversibility) below which Sam ships without sign-off and above which the council reviews. Restoring discretion on the routine majority of work returns ownership where it costs the council nothing.
- *Owner: Sam's manager.* Give Sam a real rationale, and a voice. The gate was imposed with no explanation Sam found persuasive; autonomy support includes a meaningful reason for a constraint and acknowledgment of the perspective it overrides. Explain *why* the council exists (the actual risk it is meant to catch) and invite Sam to help define the threshold above. A constraint Sam helped shape is far less autonomy-thwarting than one handed down.
- *Owner: Sam's manager / council.* Put Sam *on* the council, or make them the team's standing reviewer for it. The fastest way to convert "strangers overrule my calls" back into ownership is to give Sam authority within the new structure rather than only under it. This also pre-empts the relatedness drift (the council stops being a faceless out-group).

**Competence (not deficient; protect it).**
- *Owner: Sam's manager.* Make sure the gate does not start slowing Sam's shipped work to the point that effectiveness erodes. Track lead time on Sam's changes; if the council becomes a bottleneck, the autonomy fix above also protects competence.

**Relatedness (not deficient; a light touch).**
- *Owner: Sam's manager.* Keep the peer relationships warm and, via the council-seat move above, close the distance to the new body so it does not quietly erode mattering.

**Secondary need waiting behind the binding one.** If autonomy is restored and Sam re-engages, watch competence next: a re-energized senior engineer will want harder problems and real growth. Have a genuine stretch challenge ready once ownership is back, so the returning drive has somewhere to go.

---

## Re-check

Reassess in four to six weeks, after the gate has been right-sized and Sam has had real decisions back for a few cycles. Watch for: volunteering for hard problems again, richer engagement in review and design discussions, discretionary effort returning. If the signs persist after autonomy is genuinely restored (not just promised), re-run the read: the binding need may have been misjudged, or a second need (competence erosion from a slow gate, or relatedness strain from the council) may have moved. Needs interact, so supporting autonomy can change the competence and relatedness readings; this report is a snapshot, not a settled verdict.

---

## Caveats

- SDT diagnoses the **sources and quality** of motivation, not a clinical, psychiatric, or medical state. Persistent disengagement can have causes outside this frame (health, life circumstances, fit); if the autonomy fix does not move the needle, do not assume the model is the whole story.
- This read rests on **self-report and inference**, not measurement. No validated instrument was administered, and the satisfaction / frustration / bearing ratings are analyst judgment, not readings off a meter.
- You can **build conditions** that support Sam's autonomy; you cannot **manufacture** their intrinsic motivation directly. Restoring ownership raises the odds that the old drive returns; it does not command it, and pressure aimed straight at "be motivated again" would backfire.
- The three needs **interact.** This read isolates autonomy as the binding need given competence and relatedness held intact; in a case where two needs were thwarted at once the synthesis would look different.
- All names and details here are illustrative for documentation purposes and were not researched.
