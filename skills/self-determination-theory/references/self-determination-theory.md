# Self-Determination Theory: the three needs, the motivation continuum, and diagnostic prompts

Self-Determination Theory (SDT) is a theory of human motivation developed by Edward Deci and Richard Ryan at the University of Rochester, formalized in 1985, given its canonical statement in Ryan and Deci's 2000 paper, and revisited in their 25-year retrospective in 2020. Its Basic Psychological Needs Theory holds that three innate psychological needs drive autonomous motivation, performance, and wellbeing when they are satisfied, and drive disengagement and ill-being when they are thwarted. The needs are not learned preferences that vary by person; the theory treats them as universal nutrients, like food or water for the mind.

The diagnostic question this skill answers is not "is motivation high or low" but "which need is being thwarted, and how do we support it." A person can look unmotivated for three quite different reasons, and the fix for each is different.

## The three needs

### Autonomy: volition and ownership

Experiencing one's actions as self-endorsed, chosen, and flowing from one's own values and interests. Autonomy is not independence or doing whatever you want; it is acting with a sense of willingness even within constraints.

- **Supported by:** offering choice, inviting voice and input, giving a meaningful rationale for requests, minimizing controlling language, acknowledging the person's perspective and feelings.
- **Thwarted by:** control, pressure, surveillance, micromanagement, externally imposed goals, deadlines and rewards used as levers, "because I said so."

### Competence: effectiveness and growth

Experiencing oneself as effective, capable of producing valued outcomes, and growing in skill. The need is met not by easy success but by mastering something that stretched you.

- **Supported by:** clear structure and expectations, challenge calibrated to current skill (hard enough to engage, not so hard it defeats), useful and specific feedback, visible progress, tools that fit the work.
- **Thwarted by:** tasks far too hard or far too easy, no feedback or only negative feedback, moving targets, repeated failure, being set up to fail.

### Relatedness: belonging and mattering

Experiencing warmth, connection, and the sense that one matters to others and belongs in the group. The need is for being cared about, not just being liked.

- **Supported by:** genuine connection, inclusion, being known and valued as a person, warmth from those who matter, contributing to something shared.
- **Thwarted by:** isolation, exclusion, being treated as interchangeable or purely transactional, cold or instrumental relationships, contribution going unseen.

## Satisfaction and frustration are two readings, not one scale

Do not collapse each need onto a single high-to-low axis. Read each need twice:

- **Satisfaction:** to what degree is the need actively met here.
- **Frustration:** to what degree is the need actively thwarted or undermined here.

These are distinct. A need can be neither well satisfied nor actively frustrated (simply neglected), and that is a different, milder state than active thwarting. Active frustration (thwarting) is more damaging than mere low satisfaction: a thwarted need produces defensiveness, withdrawal, and ill-being, not just a lack of energy. When you score, the most thwarted need usually drives the problem more than the least satisfied one. Weight frustration accordingly.

## Motivation quality: the continuum

SDT's second core claim is that motivation is about quality, not only amount. Two people can work equally hard for opposite reasons, and the reason predicts persistence, creativity, and wellbeing. The organismic integration continuum runs from controlled to autonomous:

| | Type | What drives it |
|---|---|---|
| **Amotivation** | none | no intention to act; sees no point, feels incapable |
| **Controlled** | external regulation | external rewards and punishments; "I'll get paid / I'll be fired" |
| **Controlled** | introjected regulation | internal pressure: guilt, shame, ego, shoulds; "I'd feel like a failure if I didn't" |
| **Autonomous** | identified regulation | personal value: "this matters to me, even if I don't enjoy it" |
| **Autonomous** | integrated regulation | aligned with identity and other values: "this is who I am" |
| **Autonomous** | intrinsic motivation | inherent interest and enjoyment: "I do this because I love it" |

Satisfying the three needs moves motivation toward the autonomous end. Thwarting them pushes it back toward the controlled end and, at the extreme, toward amotivation. Controlled motivation can still produce output, often for a while, but it costs persistence, creativity, and wellbeing, and it collapses when the external pressure is removed. The aim of a need-supportive intervention is not "more motivation" but motivation of a more autonomous kind.

## Diagnostic prompts

Use these to read each need in a real situation. They are original, generic prompts keyed to the three needs, written for diagnosis, not for scoring against any validated instrument. Adapt the wording to the context (work, study, a team, oneself).

**Autonomy**
- Does the person act because they choose to, or because they feel pushed, watched, or controlled?
- Do they have real say over how (and ideally what) they do? Or are the goals and methods imposed?
- When asked to do something, are they given a reason that makes sense to them, or just told?
- Has a recent change taken away discretion they used to have?

**Competence**
- Does the person feel effective at this, and see themselves getting better?
- Is the challenge calibrated: stretching but achievable, rather than trivial or crushing?
- Do they get clear, useful feedback and see their progress, or do they work in the dark?
- Have they been set up to succeed (clear expectations, the right tools), or to fail?

**Relatedness**
- Does the person feel they belong here and matter to the people around them?
- Are they known and valued as a person, or treated as interchangeable and transactional?
- Is their contribution seen and cared about, or invisible?
- Are they included, or isolated and on the outside?

## On instruments and Daniel Pink's "Drive"

Validated self-report instruments exist for these needs, most notably the Basic Psychological Need Satisfaction and Frustration Scale (BPNSFS) and work-specific need-satisfaction scales. Do not reproduce their items. This skill uses original, generic diagnostic prompts keyed to the three needs, not any scored questionnaire.

Daniel Pink's popular book *Drive* (autonomy, mastery, purpose) is a well-known restatement of SDT for a general audience. It is a useful entry point, but it simplifies and relabels the theory. This skill follows the validated theory, with relatedness as the third need rather than "purpose," and with the satisfaction-versus-frustration distinction the popular version omits.
